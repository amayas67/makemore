Study notes — probability & deep learning

Probability Foundations for Deep Learning

Nine building blocks, in dependency order, that explain why torch.randn, the × 0.01 scaling, and the Xavier/Kaiming ÷ √n rule in the MLP init code are not arbitrary — each one follows from the one before it.

01

What is a random variable?

A random variable is just a variable whose value comes from a random experiment, together with the information of how likely each outcome is. It is not mysterious — it is a number you don't know yet, but whose possible values and odds you do know.

example

torch.randint(1, 7, (1,))  # one value in {1, ..., 6}, all equally likely
a bit more formally A random variable is a function \(X\) that assigns a number to each outcome of an experiment. The die roll is the experiment; \(X\) reads off which face landed. What makes it "random" is not the function — it's that the input (which face lands) is not fixed in advance.
02

What is a distribution?

A distribution answers: with what probability does each value occur? For a die, it's the list of six probabilities. Below are 1000 simulated die rolls, binned — this is what a distribution looks like when you actually sample it instead of assuming it.

live sample — 1000 die rolls
continuous case, one subtlety For a continuous variable (like the normal distribution below), asking "what's the probability of exactly 0.532871…" gives zero — there are infinitely many possible values. What we describe instead is a density: probability is the area under the curve over an interval, not the height at a point. This matters later — it's why the normal "distribution" is written as a formula for a curve, not a table of probabilities.
03

What is the uniform distribution?

The distribution where every outcome is equally likely. The die above is the discrete case. A continuous uniform variable — e.g. torch.rand(...) — picks any real number in \([0,1)\) with equal density everywhere in that range. It's the "flattest" distribution: no value is favored over another.

04

What is the normal distribution?

A specific, non-uniform distribution: values near the center are common, values far from the center are rare, and the rate of drop-off is smooth and symmetric.

\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \, e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \]

You can set this formula aside at first — the important content is the shape. Below is a live histogram of 5000 samples drawn from a standard normal:

live sample — \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\), 5000 draws
05

What is the mean?

The center of the distribution — the value the data balances around. For torch.randn, \(\mu = 0\): values are just as likely to land above 0 as below it.

06

What are variance and standard deviation?

Both measure spread around the mean, but they're not interchangeable:

\[ \text{Var}(X) = E[(X-\mu)^2] \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)} \]

Variance is in squared units (if \(X\) is in meters, \(\text{Var}(X)\) is in meters²) — that's a mathematical convenience for the algebra in section 9, but it's not directly interpretable as "distance from the mean." Standard deviation, the square root, brings it back to the original units, which is why it's the one people picture as "typical spread."

Computing it by hand, step by step:

  1. Compute the mean of the values.
  2. Subtract the mean from every value — this gives how far each one sits from the center.
  3. Square every difference. Squaring stops positive and negative differences from cancelling out, and it punishes large deviations more than small ones.
  4. Average these squared differences. That average is the variance.
values 2, 4, 6 1. mean μ = (2 + 4 + 6) / 3 = 4 2. differences from the mean 2 − 4 = −2    4 − 4 = 0    6 − 4 = 2 3. squared differences (−2)² = 4    0² = 0    2² = 4 4. variance = average of the squared differences (4 + 0 + 4) / 3 = 2.67
important Variance is not the average distance from the mean — it's the average of the squared distances. That gap between "distance" and "squared distance" is exactly why a square root shows up next, to bring things back to a number you can picture directly.

And concretely, taking that square root: if \(\text{Var}(X) = 9\), then \(\sigma = \sqrt{9} = 3\) — values are typically about 3 units from the mean. Same information, just back in the original unit instead of squared units.

Same sample size, two different \(\sigma\) — notice how much more the wide one spills into the tails:

07

What does torch.randn actually do?

It draws samples from \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\): mean 0, standard deviation 1. Nothing more exotic than that — it's literally sampling the curve from section 4.

08

Why multiply by 0.01, like in W2 = torch.randn(...) * 0.01?

Scaling a random variable by a constant \(c\) scales its standard deviation by \(c\) and its variance by \(c^2\):

\[ \text{if } X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2), \text{ then } cX \sim \mathcal{N}(c\mu,\, c^2\sigma^2) \]

So randn(...) * 0.01 keeps the shape (still normal, still centered at 0) but squeezes it: \(\mathcal{N}(0,1) \rightarrow \mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)\). Every sample lands about 100× closer to 0 than before. This is used for W2 specifically so that the initial logits are small — large random logits at step 0 make the initial loss unnecessarily high and the first gradients noisy.

09

Why divide by \(\sqrt{n}\)? (Xavier / Kaiming initialization)

This part is a consequence of linear algebra and how variance behaves under sums — not probability theory in the same sense as sections 1–8.

A neuron's pre-activation is a weighted sum:

\[ z = x_1 w_1 + x_2 w_2 + \cdots + x_n w_n \]
the step the original plan skipped The claim "more terms → more variance, roughly as \(n\)" is true, but it needs one more building block to actually derive: the variance of a product of two independent, zero-mean variables is the product of their variances. If \(x_i\) and \(w_i\) are independent with \(E[x_i]=E[w_i]=0\), \(\text{Var}(x_i) = \sigma_x^2\), \(\text{Var}(w_i)=\sigma_w^2\), then: \[ \text{Var}(x_i w_i) = \sigma_x^2 \sigma_w^2 \] This is the missing link between "products of random things" and "sums of variances" — without it, the \(n \cdot \sigma_x^2\sigma_w^2\) step is asserted rather than derived.

Now sum \(n\) such independent terms — variances of independent variables add:

\[ \text{Var}(z) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(x_i w_i) = n\,\sigma_x^2\sigma_w^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sigma_z = \sigma_x\,\sigma_w\sqrt{n} \]

The standard deviation of the neuron's output grows with \(\sqrt{n}\) purely because you're adding up more independent terms — regardless of what distribution \(x\) and \(w\) individually follow. To keep \(\sigma_z \approx \sigma_x\) (the signal doesn't blow up or shrink as it passes through the layer), you need \(\sigma_w \sqrt{n} \approx 1\), i.e.:

\[ \sigma_w \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \]

which is exactly why weights are initialized as torch.randn(...) / sqrt(n) (Xavier/Glorot) or * sqrt(2/n) (Kaiming/He — the extra factor of 2 corrects for ReLU discarding half the signal, which doesn't apply to your tanh layer).
