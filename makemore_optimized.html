Makemore Part 3 - First Optimizations

After building a basic MLP in Makemore Part 2, Karpathy begins to improve the training process. The architecture does not change yet. Instead, he focuses on making optimization easier.

1. Initializing W2 with a Small Scale

W2 = torch.randn((n_hidden, vocab_size)) * 0.01

Instead of using the raw values produced by torch.randn(), every weight is multiplied by 0.01.

Why?

The output logits are computed as:

logits = h @ W2 + b2

If W2 contains large values, the logits can immediately become large.

The softmax applies an exponential:

softmax(x) = exp(x) / Σ exp(x)

The exponential amplifies differences. Large logits produce extremely confident probabilities even before learning.

Example

Logits:

[5.2, -2.8, 1.4]

↓

exp()

[181, 0.06, 4.05]

↓

Softmax

[0.978, 0.0003, 0.021]

The network is already almost certain about one class despite having learned nothing. This is undesirable.

By multiplying W2 by 0.01, the logits remain close to zero. 

Logits:

[0.03, -0.02, 0.01]

↓

exp()

[1.030, 0.980, 1.010]

↓

Softmax

[0.34, 0.32, 0.34]

The initial probabilities become much more uniform. The network starts without any strong preference, which is a much healthier initialization.

Important: The multiplication by 0.01 does not change the fact that the weights follow a normal distribution. Instead of N(0,1) the weights now follow approximately N(0,0.01²) The distribution is simply much narrower around zero.

2. Tanh Compresses Activations: The Standard Deviation Problem

tanh causes two related problems, not just one. The vanishing gradient (below) is the second. This is the first, and it happens even before anything saturates.

Every application of tanh squeezes values into the range (-1, 1). That means it doesn't just reshape the data — it narrows its spread, i.e. its standard deviation.

Example

Before tanh (std ≈ 1):

-2.1
-0.8
 0.3
 1.7

↓ tanh

After tanh:

-0.97
-0.66
 0.29
 0.94

The values are pulled inward. Do this once and it's minor. Do it layer after layer, and the effect compounds: 

Layer 1 : std ≈ 1
   ↓ tanh
Layer 2 : std ≈ 0.7
   ↓ tanh
Layer 3 : std ≈ 0.5
   ↓ tanh
Layer 4 : std ≈ 0.3

Each layer's activations end up narrower than the layer before it. In a deep enough network, activations would shrink toward zero even if no single tanh call were anywhere near saturated.

This is what the gain compensates for. In an initialization like torch.randn(...) * (5/3) / n**0.5, the 5/3 is tanh's specific correction factor. It slightly boosts the weight scale so that the shrinking effect of tanh is cancelled out — keeping the standard deviation of activations roughly constant from layer to layer instead of decaying.
Why this barely shows up in our own network. The layer-1-to-4 cascade above is illustrative of a deep network with several stacked tanh layers. Our Makemore MLP only has a single hidden layer — one tanh call between W1 and W2. With just one layer, there's nothing to compound: getting the gain slightly wrong changes the scale of that one activation, but there's no repeated shrinking across depth to correct for. That's why tweaking the init here barely moves the loss — the effect becomes visible (and eventually critical) in networks with many stacked layers, where a small per-layer shrinkage compounds into a near-zero signal by the last layer.

3. Tanh Saturation and the Vanishing Gradient

The hidden layer uses the tanh activation: 

h = torch.tanh(...)

Its derivative is:

tanh'(x) = 1 − tanh²(x)

If we denote

t = tanh(x)

then

dt/dx = 1 − t²

What happens near ±1?

Suppose 

tanh(x) = 0.999

Then

1 − 0.999² ≈ 0.002

The derivative is almost zero.

Backpropagation

During backpropagation the chain rule multiplies gradients. 

Incoming gradient

1

×

Derivative of tanh

0.002

=

Outgoing gradient

0.002

Only a tiny gradient reaches the previous operations.

The weights before tanh therefore receive almost no update:

W = W − learning_rate × gradient

If the gradient is almost zero, the weight update is almost zero as well.

This phenomenon is called Vanishing Gradient. As the gradient travels backward through the network, it becomes smaller and smaller, making earlier layers learn extremely slowly.

Why does initialization matter?

If the weights are initialized with very large values, the input of tanh becomes large as well. 

x = 7

↓

tanh(x) ≈ 1

↓

Derivative ≈ 0

The neuron starts immediately in the saturated region. Learning becomes difficult from the very first iteration.

A better initialization keeps the activations close to zero, where tanh behaves almost linearly. 

x = 0

↓

tanh(0) = 0

↓

Derivative = 1

This allows gradients to flow much more easily through the network.

How the Two Problems Connect

These aren't two separate issues — they're the same root cause, seen at two different scales.

If activations get too large, for example: 

-8
 5
12

then tanh pushes almost everything to the extremes: 

-1
 1
 1

It's fully saturated. And once saturated, both problems show up at once:

  • the activations are all pinned near ±1 — this is the standard deviation problem, taken to its extreme;
  • the derivative is nearly 0 — this is the vanishing gradient.
Controlling the variance of activations is what prevents tanh from saturating in the first place.

This is exactly why Karpathy spends so much effort on weight initialization: the goal is for activations, right from the first training step, to sit in the nearly-linear region of tanh around zero — not because that region is special in itself, but because staying there avoids both problems at once.

tanh's shrinking effect (problem 1) → pushes activations toward saturation saturation → derivative ≈ 0 → vanishing gradient (problem 2)

In one sentence: tanh compresses activations into (-1, 1), and if the inputs are too large it saturates — which sharply shrinks gradients and slows or blocks learning. Initialization techniques like Xavier/Kaiming, together with a tanh-specific gain like 5/3, exist to keep activations in the range where tanh still works well.

4. Normalizing hpreact by Hand: A Preview of Batch Normalization

Initialization only controls the variance of activations at the start of training. Karpathy takes it one step further: he actively normalizes hpreact during the forward pass itself.

The shape of hpreact

hpreact.shape = (batch_size, n_hidden)

# e.g.
hpreact.shape = (32, 200)
  • 32 rows → the 32 contexts (examples) in the mini-batch.
  • 200 columns → the 200 hidden neurons.

A concrete example: one neuron, three contexts

ContextNeuron 1 activation
"emma"-2
"john"0
"lucas"2

Neuron 1 produced [-2, 0, 2] across these 3 contexts. We compute:

mean: μ = (-2 + 0 + 2) / 3 = 0 std: σ ≈ 2

(more precisely, σ = √(8/3) ≈ 1.63 — the "≈ 2" above is a loose rounding for intuition, it doesn't change the reasoning below.)

We then normalize this neuron's outputs, across the 3 contexts.

With two neurons: normalization works column by column

ContextNeuron 1Neuron 2
A-250
B060
C270

Batch normalization treats each column independently: the neuron 1 column gets its own mean, std, and normalization; the neuron 2 column gets its own, separately. That's why Karpathy writes: 

hpreact.mean(0)

Averaging over dimension 0 means averaging over the batch dimension (the rows) — independently, per column. Neurons are never compared against each other.

Why does Karpathy actually do this? The connection to tanh

Without normalization — suppose the raw pre-activations look like: 

-15
  8

-10

...

Applying h = torch.tanh(hpreact):

tanh(-15) ≈ -1 tanh(8) ≈ 1 tanh(20) ≈ 1

Almost every neuron comes out saturated at ±1, and the derivative 1 - tanh²(x) collapses to nearly 0. Gradients vanish — same problem as sections 2–3, just triggered by the raw scale of the pre-activations instead of a bad initialization.

With normalization — Karpathy does: 

hpreact = (hpreact - mean) / std

The same raw values above might become something like: 

-1.5
 0.3
 1.2
-0.9
 0.8

Now:

tanh(-1.5) = -0.90 tanh(0.3) = 0.29 tanh(1.2) = 0.83

Neurons are no longer all pinned to ±1. Their derivatives stay reasonably large, and gradients flow much better through the network.

The goal here isn't "nicer statistics." It's a concrete engineering target: keep activations out of tanh's saturation zone.

Why subtract the mean?

If a column is:

Why divide by the standard deviation?

Take two neurons, both already centered on 0. The first produces [-2, 0, 2]; the second produces [-200, 0, 200]. Even though both are centered on 0, the second will fully saturate tanh regardless. Dividing by its own standard deviation: 

-1
 0
 1

brings both neurons to a comparable scale.

In one sentence: the point of hpreact = (hpreact - mean) / std is to make sure every neuron sends tanh values centered around 0 with a controlled spread (std ≈ 1), so tanh operates in its nearly-linear region, its derivative stays high, and gradients propagate efficiently during training. This hand-written normalization is exactly the idea that gives rise to Batch Normalization.

A surprising side effect: examples become coupled across the batch

Because normalization divides by hpreact.mean(0) and hpreact.std(0), computed over the whole mini-batch, a given example's normalized activation is no longer a function of that example alone. It also depends on whichever other examples happen to share its batch on that particular step.

Concretely: the exact same context can produce a slightly different hpreact depending on whether it lands in a batch where the other 31 examples happen to pull the batch's mean and std one way or another. Run the same input through the network twice, in two different batches, and the output can differ.

Karpathy's point: it looks like a bug, but it acts as a regularizer. Since each example's representation gets jittered depending on its random batch companions at every step, the network can't simply memorize a fixed output for a given input — it's forced to be somewhat robust to that jitter. This has the same flavor as other regularization techniques (dropout, data augmentation): injecting a bit of noise/entropy during training tends to reduce overfitting, as a side effect of something that was introduced to solve a completely different problem (saturation).
One cause, not two. Without batch normalization, an MLP processes every row of the batch completely independently — which other examples happen to sit next to it never affects its output. Shuffling the batch would change nothing for a given example. It is specifically batch normalization that opens the channel through which batch composition can affect an example's output, by folding mean(0)/ std(0) over the whole batch into the computation. So the model can't behave like a giant fixed lookup table (same input → always the same output) — but the reason is BN, not "the batch" as some separate factor.

This is also only true during training, while mean(0)/ std(0) are computed live on the current batch. Once bnmean_running/bnstd_running are used instead (fixed values, at eval/generation time), that channel closes and the network becomes a deterministic function of its input again — the only randomness left in the generation loop is torch.multinomial, which is an unrelated sampling step (picking the next character from the predicted probabilities), not an effect of batch normalization.

The catch: this breaks down at inference time

At generation time, examples are processed one at a time (batch size 1). Computing hpreact.mean(0) over a single example just returns that example itself — so subtracting the mean zeroes out every activation. And hpreact.std(0) over a single example is undefined: PyTorch's .std() divides by n - 1 by default, so with n = 1 that's a division by zero, giving NaN.

This is exactly why Karpathy introduces running estimates of the mean and standard deviation (commonly named bnmean_running and bnstd_running), updated with a small momentum term after every training step. At test/generation time, the network uses these running statistics instead of recomputing batch statistics on the fly — sidestepping both the batch-size-1 crash above and the batch-coupling effect described in this section.

The concrete fix: running mean/std, replacing the on-the-fly stats

Three changes. Nothing about batch normalization itself changes — only where mean/std come from.

1. Create two buffers once, before training — not learned via backprop, just running records:

bnmean_running = torch.zeros((1, n_hidden))
bnstd_running  = torch.ones((1, n_hidden))

2. During training, after computing the batch's own mean/std to normalize hpreact as before, update the running buffers with a moving average:

bnmeani = hpreact.mean(0, keepdim=True)
bnstdi  = hpreact.std(0, keepdim=True)
hpreact = bngain * (hpreact - bnmeani) / bnstdi + bnbias

with torch.no_grad():
    bnmean_running = 0.999 * bnmean_running + 0.001 * bnmeani
    bnstd_running  = 0.999 * bnstd_running  + 0.001 * bnstdi

3. In split_loss and in generation, replace the on-the-fly statistics with the running ones:

# before
h = bngain * (h - h.mean(0, keepdim=True)) / h.std(0, keepdim=True) + bnbias

# after
h = bngain * (h - bnmean_running) / bnstd_running + bnbias
Why this specifically fixes the NaN. h.std(0) computes Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / (n − 1) on whatever tensor is currently in hand. At generation time, h has shape (1, n_hidden) — one row (one context), 200 columns (200 neurons), each column handled independently. For any single column, that's one value: the sum of squared deviations is always exactly 0 (a lone point never deviates from its own mean), and n − 1 = 0. 0 / 0 is undefined — PyTorch returns NaN, once per neuron, for all 200 columns at once. bnstd_running never runs that formula on the current tensor at all — it's a number already computed and stored during training. Reading a stored constant can't divide by the current batch's (n − 1), whatever that happens to be.

Why bnstd_running is a good stand-in for "the population"

A single batch's bnstdi is just an estimate — computed from 32 randomly sampled contexts, it fluctuates from step to step purely from sampling noise, even though the true underlying spread of that neuron's activations across the whole dataset doesn't actually change that fast.

bnstd_running ← 0.999 · bnstd_running + 0.001 · bnstdi

bnstd_running is an exponential moving average of thousands of these noisy per-batch estimates. Each update only nudges the running value 0.1% of the way toward the current batch's estimate. Averaged over thousands of steps, the batch-to-batch sampling noise cancels out (some batches overshoot, some undershoot, and it washes out), while the real signal — the neuron's actual typical spread, which changes slowly as W1/b1 converge — survives and accumulates. What's left is a stable estimate of the population statistic: roughly what this neuron's standard deviation would be if you computed it over the entire training set at once, instead of one noisy batch at a time.

That's exactly why it works at inference: bnstd_running doesn't need any examples in the current forward pass to be meaningful — it was never describing "this batch" to begin with, it already describes the dataset as a whole. A batch of size 1 (or of 10,000) is irrelevant to it, because it was never derived from the current batch in the first place.

Key Takeaways

  • Initializing W2 with *0.01 keeps the initial logits small.
  • Small logits produce nearly uniform probabilities.
  • Every application of tanh shrinks the standard deviation of activations, even before anything saturates — and this compounds across layers.
  • The 5/3 gain used in tanh-based initialization exists specifically to counteract this shrinking.
  • The derivative of tanh is 1 - tanh²(x).
  • When tanh saturates near ±1, its derivative approaches zero.
  • Very small derivatives produce the Vanishing Gradient.
  • Good initialization helps keep neurons away from saturation during the first training iterations.
  • Normalizing hpreact per neuron (hpreact.mean(0), column by column) keeps activations centered and at a controlled scale throughout training, not just at initialization.
  • This hand-written (hpreact - mean) / std step is the direct ancestor of Batch Normalization.
  • Because the mean/std are computed over the whole batch, examples get coupled together — the same input can produce a slightly different activation depending on its batch companions. This acts as an (accidental) regularizer during training, but breaks at batch-size-1 inference, which is why running mean/std estimates are needed at test/generation time.
